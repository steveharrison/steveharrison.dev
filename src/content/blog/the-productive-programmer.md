Everyone agrees good habits are essential, but what are these good habits?

## 1. Being in an effective state to work.

Energy - both mental and physical. Starting a new gym plan while starting a new job not a great idea, work takes energy, even if mental energy, and that reduces the amount of energy you can put into other things. Likewise, if you've used up all your energy in a workout, you have less to put into work. You gotta strike a balance between health and productivity (of course, some exercise helps productivity, but there's a balance). When I work out, I want more sleep, and am less inclined to put in extra hours.

Regardless of what work you need to complete, if you missed your alarm, arrive at work late without having breakfast, and know you need to squeeze in a haircut at lunch for a date that evening, you’re probably not going to have the most productive day.

No matter how talented and organised you are with your work, you need to be in a state where you can reasonably complete the work. You want to be happy, healthy, be in the right environment, and have the right amount of time to complete said work. If you've had a terrible sleep, even working on the most exciting stuff can be an absolute slog. Likewise, if you feel great, you can make a lot of headway with something mind-numbingly boring. Of course, life's not perfect, and you will have to push through and complete work when you're not in an ideal state sometimes. However, the aim is to make it as enjoyable as possible to do the work and minimise the times you have to push through.

### Health

#### Physical

*Get a good sleep.* Easier said than done, I know. Have a good bed + pillow, be a comfortable temperature, don't eat 2 hours before bed, have a quiet + dark environment, allow yourself enough time so that you would wake up without an alarm (it's not a fixed duration like 8h -- it varies depending on you and how much energy you've been using), and above all, do what relaxes you in the evening.

*Supplement sleep with naps.* If you've had a bad sleep, having a 10-30min nap at lunch time or mid-afternoon can be great to pick up energy again. Much more effective than coffee/exercise/snacking if you are tired. And if you are in a slump, a quick nap until you're feeling recharged can work great too. I'd much rather work a little later while having energy to do so, than to push through the day and end up at 5:30pm wrecked.

*Eat well.* - Eating well sustains energy throughout the day and helps you to feel good so that you can focus. Eating badly causes you to have more slumps and distractions because you're not feeling as good.

For me, a Mediterranean diet and using high-quality Organic ingredients work really well. I like having enough protein + carbs + veggies/salad to be satisfied. And don't under/over-eat: you shouldn't be hungry/bloated after every meal, otherwise this discomfort will be a distraction. I find I'm most productive after a meal and a little walk, as my energy is good and I'm ready to sit down and focus for a while.

Generally: buying ingredients from health food stores and cooking for yourself is always going to be healthier than eating out, even if you're buying Salmon + Soba noodle takeaway salads from the trendiest health food joints in town. Plus, it's cheaper and gives you an activity to do to break up your screen time!

A well-stocked pantry with healthy snacks is great too. A piece of fruit / some nuts / dried fruit for morning and afternoon tea help provide breaks, rewards, and energy when you’re starting to fade.

Know when to take a day off to just focus on recovering. #### Mental

*Be happy.* Again, easier said than done. But understand that whatever is going on in your life will affect your productivity. If you can avoid stress and take care of everything in your personal life, you will be in much better headspace for work and not be distracted. I find I'm much more productive after a fun weekend spending quality time with friends/family, or a holiday.

*Take care of loose threads in your personal life.* If you haven't figured out how much travel time you need to that event after work, or haven't booked tickets to that new release movie you want to go see but will be very popular, these things niggle in the back of your mind and are distractions. So don't procrastinate in your personal life—make sure this stuff is taken care of so that when you start work, you can relax and not worry about anything else.

*Ensure that you have sufficient breaks from coding and have variety in your life.* By Friday, my body and mind is usually more tired than on Monday. But if I have a weekend coding sesh on my side projects, then I might not have had the refresh I need. Doing something enjoyable but different from work on weekends / in the evening can really help avoid fatigue / burnout. However, as an introvert, I've found a lot of the activities I want to do overlap with work. I've found that writing/using whiteboards/reading provide a welcome break from screen time, and a few activities like running/golf/walking in nice areas.

### Environment

Having an ideal environment to do your work in is the prerequisite to enjoyable work. It should be somewhere that you _want_ to spend a few hours sitting/standing.

*Get a good desk, chair, and monitor.* It honestly makes such a difference. I used to think that I couldn't be productive at home, but it turned out it was mostly because I was trying to use my laptop on the couch.
A nice standup desk + chair make a huge difference. I’ve had the luxury of buying a solid wood standup desk, which brings me joy every day. It also has a range of 60mm-120mm, allowing me to get it at exactly the height that feels comfortable at that time (a lot of standup desks don’t go this low and this restrict your options). Whenever my legs start feeling cramped but I still want to remain in the zone for a bit longer, I’ll move it into standing. It’s worth noting, however, that I never feel as much in “the zone” when I’m standing compared to sitting. So I would always choose going for a walk then sitting for a bit, over not going for a walk and standing. However, it’s great for some variety / getting through meetings when you can’t necessarily just go for a walk. A co-worker has even bought a treadmill under his desk that he uses at standups. If I get one, I’ll update this article with my findings. :)

Stay away from Email and Teams. These things are incredibly bad for your focus. Do Not Disturb + Muting channels are your friends.

*Breathe fresh air.* Fresh air, sunshine, nature all have a huge impact on health and mood. It's not surprising that a lot of the tech giant campuses in Silicon Valley have this taken care of. Ensure there is good airflow in your room. This impacts comfort, but also fresh air directly impacts your brain’s ability to focus.

*Lighting* Working in a dim area is going to be a pain and make you feel like you want to escape / go for breaks. You want the place you're working to be the place you want to be.

Being close to somewhere that’s pleasant to walk is great too. Living in the bush at my parents’ place in summer is great for this: I can just go for a stroll amongst the trees whenever I need a breather.

*Have enough time.* You also want to have enough time to do the tasks you need to do. Not having commitments like meetings / errands to run over lunch / events you have to be at in the evening allows you less stress that you won't have enough time for the task at hand. Of course, you need to make this work for your life as well.

## 3. Doing the work itself effectively

Even if everything else is optimised, how you approach the work affects how much you get done. The following helps:
- Be organised—this will flow through to everything.
- Look for optimisations and remember that they accumulate.
- Not all tasks are equal—slotting the right tasks at the right time is essential.
- Not all multi-tasking is bad—while you need to focus sometimes, some tasks have natural space like waiting for a build, waiting on someone for test data, etc. where you can work on smaller tasks in between. This also alleviates boredom with a dry task.

## Time
- Being on time
- Removing distractions, like meetings or any other personal commitments.
- Having an appropriate amount of time to complete your work.

*Be organised.*

It seems like being organised is no longer a pre-requesite to have a job. We're so used to things that save us when we're disorganised, like UberEats, that we've forgotten previous generations were much more organised because they had to be to survive. My parents only go into town twice a week, so this automatically forces them to be organised with planning meals. Now that being organised is so rare, if you only improve in this area slightly, you'll be miles ahead of everyone else.

Examples of being organised:
- Write a note to yourself on Friday noting what you achieved that day, so when it’s your turn at standup on Monday, you’re not one of the many people who laughs “Gee, I’ve forgotten what I did last week!” Haha. You can have a good weekend and still give a coherent standup update: not doing this simply means you’re disorganised.
- Using a ToDo list to keep track of tasks you need to complete that day (yep, separate to Jira). I love Things.
- Planning your day around your meetings.
- Reaching out to people as soon as possible to get a requirement, so that when you pick up a task, you know everything you need to get started.
- Using Bookmarks and Bookmark folders effectively. It’s fine to not know where something is once, but you should never struggle to find it a second time. If you are going to access something again, bookmark it! Here are some example bookmark folders/bookmarks I have:
	- Feature X
		- Figma
		- Jira
		- GitHub PR
		- Relevant StackOverflow post
		- Localhost link with test query string params
	- Jira
	- Confluence
	- Team Leave Calendar
	- Timesheet portal
- Use different Chrome profiles if you work for multiple clients—that way you have one bookmark set per client.
- Organising folders on your computer so you can find projects easily.
- Making use of `.npmrc` to repeat simple Terminal commands (separate article on this).
- The simple act of writing is great to organise your thoughts. From keeping a notebook/pieces of paper and writing a schedule of what you’ll do that day to using it to sketch out programming logic, it varies up screen time and organises your mind.
- Doing tasks in parallel if appropriate: you can ask dependencies from multiple people, then get them to deliver in time for when you need something. If you’re Clauding/doing a build/running some low-running Cypress tests/etc, may as well have it working while you’re on a break / at lunch.
- Getting dependencies for future tasks.
- Don’t switch back and forth between Git branches. Having to wipe node modules / etc. really time-consuming/cognitive load.
- If there are some tasks that you need to get done today, like preparing for a demo, submitting your timesheet, etc. you get that done first. Record the screen recording of your demo, and then you can sink into the deep work tasks.

*Good decisions accumulate.* the clickable cards I added and various dev improvement things like the modal have been consistently useful every day. But could have easily “not bothered” too and then every day I’d be getting frustrated by the same issues.

### Look for optimisations to make doing a task the second time faster than the first.

Earlier this year, I took an hour or so to redesign our test flight booking page to make the cards more clickable, and to add a range of mock bookings we can select from (e.g. Domestic flight, International Flight, Flight with 3 passengers). I also added an alert that appears if we don't have the mock server running. This has saved myself and teammates significant effort over the past year, and it pays dividends every day. It’s not just a time saving, it’s also psychological and reducing cognitive load later on. You want to make doing good work as easy and enjoyable as possible, and if you can add optimisations that give you a moment of joy, this gives you a mental boost every time you get to use your clever work. This compounds.

Even if something doesn’t save that much time, if it reduces cognitive load (like a bookmark rather than having to type something into Confluence to find it), this accumulates over time to make doing tasks a breeze rather than having to overcome all these perceived obstacles to get started.

Be the person who says “Wow, past me, I’m glad I took the time to do that—it’s made my life so much easier!” not “Gee, I really should get around to doing that but I just can’t be bothered right now”.

### Not all tasks are equal

The first critical thing is to understand that not all tasks are equal.

The most typical programming tasks require deep work: focusing for an extended period of time (3 hours for example) and removing all distractions. This might be creating a new feature in an app. These are typically the most enjoyable tasks for programmers as they allow you to enter a state of flow. There are also tasks like fixing bugs that may not be that enjoyable or allow you to get into the flow, but will typically take a similar amount of focused time.

Then there are simple tasks like changing the colour of a button or fixing minor bugs. These require a lot less thought and can be done reasonably quickly (15min for example).

However, there are many other tasks in the work day of a typical programmer. For example, most companies have daily standups, a meeting that’s usually 15min or so in the morning where everyone shares what they’re working on. If you’re following Agile methodologies, there might also be many other meetings throughout the week.

Then there is code review. Simple code reviews you can read fairly easily and then give feedback and/or Approve. For more complex code reviews, however, you're going to want to run it locally yourself and understand how they’ve solved particular problems—this typically requires a lot more time and focus.

Some things are open-ended and frustrating: it can be good to time box these and push them back into less productive time slots so you get the other stuff out of the way first and have made some progress.

Different tasks require different conditions.

How to handle boring tasks:
- Pair them with something satisfying, like food.
- Do something fun / interesting before, or in between, like learning a new technology you want to look into.

Leaving phone away sets you up to avoid distractions.

Different meals suit different scenarios. Know how food impacts your energy as well.

With focus blocks, ideal to have them open-ended so you can work until you complete a task or start feeling tired, whichever comes first. Finishing the last 20% while you’re in the zone is crucial.

Obviously stuff like bathroom breaks. But do experiment a bit—it really helps to not break the flow.

What things can you control? You want to craft the ideal day within your constraints.

Know what things increase or decrease your productivity. Some things are the same between people, other things vary.

Your eyes need a break, your brain needs a break, your body needs a break.

This is why remote work is great, you can schedule tasks like laundry for when you naturally need an eye break / need to move. It becomes much more efficient than if you were to organise your day and be doing laundry when you’re ready to focus.

Again, you might be tempted to “get all your chores out of the way first” but something like shaving if you’re working from home—do it in a break! You don’t need much brain power for it, it’s something physical that gets your body moving, so the perfect task to combine with a bit of a rest when you’ve finished a deep work session.


Key takeaways:
- Organise your day and what tasks you approach based on type of task and your different states (deep focus, lower energy, need a break, mood, etc.). Right task for the right time/mood.
- Know what things increase/decrease your focus/energy.

A lot of things that we associate with being focused may bring a lot of focus things together. An environment that enforce focus is useful too. You can’t take meetings in the silent part of a library, so it becomes a great way to enforce focus. Things like temperature, noise, smell, distractions are managed. This is why people often like co-working spaces.

10:45am - After standup, you have a healthy snack, do some stretches, go to the bathroom, have some water, and you’re ready to get back to it.

## Multi-task

Management coaches love to blabber on about how you can only do one thing at a time. In reality, I think it’s a bit different. For your focus blocks, yes, you primarily want to be focusing on one thing. But there are often times when you may need to wait for something, whether it’s some slow-running tests, your AI copilot reasoning away, or you simply might be bored with the current task. I find having several tasks running in parallel helps with some variety. You want to have a primary task, but then you can have a secondary task that’s lower priority that you can switch to for some variety / while you’re waiting on something.

Also some tasks match different moods. If you’re working in sprints, and your productivity is measured in 2 week cycles, you can tailor which task you’re working on to your mood and the time slot you have available to do it. If you’re feeling unfocused and only have 30min until the next meeting, knocking off one of those frustrating bug fixes where you couldn’t get into the zone anyway is a perfect use for this time, and frees you up to do a more complex task in a 3 hour deep work session.


Another example is you naturally need a break from screen time during the day. Lunch is a great opportunity for this, as are little breaks in between. If you were to schedule a haircut at lunch, plus shopping, and then plan to do a 5 hour work sesh afterwards to make up, this is an unrealistic way of breaking up your time, as your energy won’t last for that long. Additionally, you will need to get up and walk, but you have nothing to combine with walking so it will just be wasting time. A better way would be to have a focused sesh in the morning, have you haircut when your energy is low and your eyes need a break, then eat lunch, get back to it now you’re refreshed and do another focus sesh. Then when you need a break, you can do the shopping, providing your eyes with a necessary break and also getting in the walking you need. Remote work is great for this, because you can schedule stuff like laundry and drying clothes as eye breaks, when the whole task couldn’t have been done at once anyway (but you’ll need to turn the alarm off because you don’t want it interrupting your focus when it’s done).


## Taking productivity further with inspiration

When I’m inspired, a task doesn’t feel like work anymore, and the entire endeavour becomes enjoyable. Times I’ve been inspired have been waking up late on a sunny Saturday and having nothing on for the day, and being able to open my laptop in my PJs looking out at a beautiful view and get stuck into learning about Coinbase’s new x402 payments system. Their website is beautiful, so my design sensibilities enjoy it, and it’s just an all-round great experience where you can eat when you feel like it and get lost in time. After Apple’s WWDC keynotes, I’d be inspired after looking at all the beautiful graphics and UIs.

Inspiration is delicate, however, and can be fleeting. While you can’t summon it on command, you can create circumstances where there is a high probability of being inspired.

It also helps to have many projects with variety. That way, in your own time, you can take one that takes your fancy. I’ve had great weekends where I dive into something that’s new and exciting.

You also want to give yourself new sources of inspiration. I find reading about entrepreneurs can really help here.

COVID changed my working life by cutting out my morning 30min commute. I started waking up at 9am rather than 7 or 8am, and my life changed. I realised that with extra sleep & rest, I felt far more inspired in my life and work.

These days, I don’t keep any commitments on Saturdays and certainly no alarm. I sleep as long as I feel like it. It’s glorious. I wake up refreshed and with so much more life force to sink into my projects.

Still, I can’t use inspiration on all of my tasks. If I need to get a feature out for my app that requires a lot of analytical grunt work in order to lay the foundation for a beautiful experience, I still don’t find doing the grunt work fun. For this, the regular productivity framework comes into use where I try to put myself in the best situation possible to do the deep work I need to do: well-rested, maybe combining it with a nice meal at a café, exercising beforehand, etc. so that I’m as comfortable as possible when doing the work. If I was to do something like this when feeling inspired, it would be a waste and I’d feel pretty uninpisred quickly.

Inspiration needs to be paired with the right activities that keep you inspired. And more importantly, you need to be able to get carried away with inspiration. The best way to harness inspiration is to get stuck into it, and all other obligations fade away. You eat when you need to / want to. You just get stuff done.

So keeping your Saturday free from any commitments, ensuring you have healthy & delicious snacks on tap, sparkling water in the fridge, not setting an alarm, and having a range of different creative outlet tasks to do increases the probability that you’ll wake up, feel refreshed, think of something you want to do, and be able to throw yourself in while enjoying the day.

The end game for productivity is an almost spiritual element where you are one with yourself and at total peace, and can then just let great work flow from you.

Interestingly enough, the no distractions element also flows through to dev tasks themselves. If you’re trying to work on a task, but suddenly something breaks in node_modules that wasn’t breaking the other day, or tests start failing unrelated to your feature, this also becomes a distraction and starts making you context switch. So these things need to be managed the same way as your physical environment: try to sort out any dependencies before your deep work session (for example, making sure that all the tests are currently passing before you begin feature work) to avoid distractions during your session.

Switching branches is the worst. You might lose node_modules/etc. Use git worktree if you have to.

Completing mandatory training, for example, would be useless to do if you’ve got a solid three hour block and can dive into some deep work. Instead, save that for when your energy is low or a busywork meeting where nothing much is happening. Or you could do it after a deep work session where you want to give your brain a break.

Don’t work on the same task for too long: otherwise you end up feeling defeated and lethargic. This is a great time to switch to a new task to get a bit of momentum, and then switch back to the old one and really get it finished.
